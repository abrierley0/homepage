Background

I graduated from the University of Glasgow with a Bachelor's degree in aeronautical engineering. Afterwards I moved to Cranfield University to complete my masters in computational fluid mechanics.

My interests are chiefly in fluid mechanics research, and particularly the area of incompressible fluid flows. I have recently written solvers focusing on vorticity formulations of the Navier-Stokes.

Experience

I decided to pursue computational fluid mechanics after recognising the challenges of and limitations to experimental work.

For my bachelor's thesis, I worked with Professor Konstantinos Kontis carrying out experimental work on high speed cavity flows at the Acre Road facillity in Glasgow.

This investigated unstable acoustic phenomena that can resonate at certain frequencies in cavities and potentially cause damage to an aircraft.

My Work

Stroke Modelling

In a group project I lead a software development team to update a fluid mechanics suite which can model blockages of blood flow in the brain and subsequently predict the perfusion of blood and aid stroke treatment. This was part of an EU-funded project which is part of the movement towards creating a digital twin of the entire human body (INSIST). This has potentially significant consequences for improving the efficiency of clinical trials for stroke treatment, which is incredibly costly. So-called in-silico or simulated clinical trials would be profitable to pharmaceutical and biomedical industries.

Vorticity Formulations

I wrote a code for modelling the flow of fluids at low speeds. This method describes the flow in terms of the vorticity, which is a measure of the local rotation of the fluid. This is in contrast to the more common approach of describing the flow in terms of the velocity and pressure. I did this using the streamfunction-vorticity formulation for two-dimensional applications, and the vector-potential vorticity formulation for 3D applications. The method requires further development, because there was an issue with divergence of vorticity. The formulations were validated using the lid driven cavity benchmark.
