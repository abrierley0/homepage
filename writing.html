21/10/2025: Nietzsche Neglected the Celts

I recently read 'Beyond Good and Evil'. Nietzsche writes that Germanic music is the soul of Europe. I agree with him.

When you say Germanic, you can consider those places were Germanic languages are spoken. That is, Austria, Switzerland, Netherlands, Belgium, Denmark, Norway, Sweden, Iceland, and the UK (English).

To say German music then, one can include Austria certainly. One may consider English music; but it is nothing near condensed like German music. And it is rather wooden. As Nietzsche says, there is no dance in it. Honestly, it's rather poor.

Nietzsche attacks the English for being too pious with their Psalms. And it is true, English classical music is nothing compared to German and Austrian classical music. Where English trumps German is the spoken and written word. Think Shakespeare and the King James Bible.

It is true then, when Nietzsche wrote in the 1880s, that Darwinism, following the discoveries of Newton, were shaking Western Christendom. Nietzsche criticised the English for being too pious. But, though he denounced anti-semitism, it is in his psychological state that he embodied the whole of Germany, as its apotheosis, representing a disturbance of the psyche that ultimately lead to Nazism. This is a people that stopped Rome. Without the constraints of God, the Germanic bow was a highly strung one, and it was inevitable it would strike. Tyranny filled the hole left by God.

Beethoven is my favourite composer. Because he was a revolutionary. Because he broke into the Romantic period from the Classical period. Because he has the restless Germanic wrath, which is well-contained and organised. You also have the pure and sacred Christian music of Bach. And the nice music of the Austrian, Mozart.

What did Nietzsche miss in his diagnosis of England? What made England different to Germany? I would like to forward the argument that the Celtic influence is underestimated. The entire West coast of these isles, and pre-Roman Britain were shaped by Celts. Celts preserved Christianity on behalf of Europe and the world throughout the dark ages, such as at Iona Abbey and in Jarrow. The Scottish Englightenment, which eventually made America, came from Celts. Scottish bankers shaped America. Scottish captains gave the British Empire Canada. The chieftain-like leadership of Scottish administrators in the Empire helped improve the world. The Industrial Revolution began in Celtic Lancashire.

And I am not saying these places were purely Celtic, but they were imbibed by its traditions. Lancashire and Scotland feel notedly pagan, compared to say Yorkshire or Bedfordshire.

It seems to me that the potency of the druids in shaping Celtic minds set foundation for Irish and Scottish monks, who educated Bede and Alcuin. Remote isles, imbued always this with something special, mysterious, and unnameable. You taste it in Islay malt. Maybe the West, the New World, is really the West of the British Isles, as well as Columbus.

Nietzsche underestimated the Celts. Yet the soul of Europe is still German. And after seeing Il Trovatore at ROH, I think Verdi is its heart.
